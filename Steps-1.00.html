Case Study 1
Executive Summary:
This case study delves into the pervasive effects of social media on mental health, illustrating how excessive usage is correlated with increased rates of depression, distractibility, and other mental health issues. Utilizing data analytics and visual tools like Power BI, we explore how targeted interventions can mitigate these adverse effects and enhance workplace productivity and personal well-being.


Background:
In the digital age, social media is ubiquitous, weaving itself into the fabric of daily life. However, its impact on mental health has been a growing concern, with studies indicating potential negative effects, particularly among heavy users.


Problem Statement:
Recent studies suggest that 5-10% of Americans could be classified as social media addicts. This addiction is strongly correlated with rising depression rates and decreased focus, both of which have profound implications for personal and professional life.


Objectives:

  • To analyze the relationship between social media usage and mental health issues.
  • To develop predictive models that identify high-risk individuals.
  • To recommend strategic interventions based on usage patterns.

Data Overview:
The data, sourced from Kaggle, includes demographics, social media usage details, and mental health metrics, quantified on a scale of 1-5 across various parameters.


Data Sources:

  • Kaggle: Social Media and Mental Health Dataset
  • Internal survey data

Data Collection Methods:
Data was collected through structured surveys and aggregated from secondary sources like academic studies and social media platforms' data analytics.


Data Features:

  • Age, Marital Status, Occupation
  • Social Media Platforms Used
  • Daily Social Media Usage Time
  • Mental Health Scores (Depression, Distractibility)

Methodology:
Analysis was performed using statistical methods and machine learning models, including regression analysis and clustering, to identify patterns and predict outcomes.


Model Selection and Development:
The best-performing model was a Bernoulli Naive Bayes classifier, chosen for its efficacy in binary classification tasks involving dichotomous feature sets.


Results and Model Performance:
The model achieved 100% accuracy on the validation set, with precision, recall, and F1-score all at 100%.


Insights and Interpretations:

  • Individuals aged 18-29 who use social media for more than 4 hours a day are significantly more distractible.
  • Light users (less than 2 hours daily) show considerably lower depression rates.

Visualizations:
Data visualizations were created using Power BI to depict the correlation between social media usage and mental health metrics.


Impact and Solution:
Proposed solutions include personalized digital detox programs for high-risk groups and preventive measures focusing on balanced usage for low-risk groups.


Business Outcomes:
Implementation of these strategies is projected to increase productivity by reducing distractibility and depression, with an ROI of $3,600 and $5,000 per individual per year for high-risk and low-risk groups, respectively.


Challenges and Solutions:

  • Data variability and underreporting were mitigated by using robust statistical techniques and model validation methods.

Lessons Learned and Future Work:
Further research is required to explore the long-term effects of reduced social media usage and its impact on broader socio-economic factors.


References:

  • Data sourced from https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/souvikahmed071/social-media-and-mental-health

This case study encapsulates the critical insights derived from the data and underscores the importance of strategic interventions to enhance mental health and productivity in the digital age.
